Welcome & Introduction

Support for Library Services: Management and Development in LIS Work

Welcome to our github highlighting the work of Library and Information Science (LIS) professionals, specifically in the Library Studies specialization, focusing on library/organization management. This project was conducted by Anna Friedman, Diana Naughton, Emma Hughes, and Sam Garcia for IS 270: Systems and Infrastructures at UCLA. In order to gain insight into the field and assess how AI is impacting LIS work, we conducted an interview with Lauren Trujillo and secondary research highlighting the profession. The following is a compiled collection and analysis of our findings.

Interview

              

Lauren Trujillo serves as the Executive Director of the Santa Barbara Public Library Foundation, where she has now worked for 7 years. She previously worked solely in fundraising for the public library in the form of capital and campaigns as Foundation Director. She later transitioned to the role of Executive Director where she "wears all the hats" including fundraising, marketing, board liaison, tech and IT, AI consultant, etc. Her department has only 3 staff members who work to raise anywhere from $1 million to $6 million each year.

                


Overview

Lauren Trujillo is the Executive Director of the Santa Barbara Public Library Foundation, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization that supports the sustainability of the Santa Barbara Public Library. A library foundation is a nonprofit that supports a library by securing financial support for its programs and services through donations, partnerships, sponsorships, endowments, community events, and other fundraising means.

Initially serving as the organization’s Fundraising Director, Lauren is now the Executive Director, running the foundation along with two other staff members. Her role falls into an administration/management category of the library studies specialization; more specifically in nonprofit management, organizational direction, fundraising, and development. While this is a management role, Lauren emphasized that her MLIS degree from UCLA has been essential in nonprofit management, including: using CRM, data management, and cataloging. The degree also teaches students how to think and problem solve, how to think about how systems and infrastructures impact people, and students with an MLIS have a hunger for learning and drive toward community values. These are crucial in community-centric fundraising when listening and gathering information and when thinking about how to integrate library values into a corporate system in fundraising.

While library students may not think about administration/management or fundraising as a profession that they aspire to work in, it is a profession that is extremely necessary, especially as the political climate works to destabilize library services. We have found that administration/management is a type of job that actually has one of the largest demands for applicants in the library studies field. Through management and development, library professionals can make a difference in protecting and supporting libraries into the future.

Typical Tasks

As an Executive Director of the Santa Barbara Public Library Foundation, Lauren’s work falls into the category of management, administration, and organizational leadership. She oversees the institution, which includes tasks such as marketing, finance, board liaising, technical oversight, and AI consulting. Her team consists of three staff members: herself, an assistant, and a development director to support fundraising. The majority of her job is focused on fundraising for the foundation in order to support the library, which includes donor relations, fiscal oversight, and organization management. Each year, her goal is to raise $1 million to $6 million for the library foundation.

According to a recent study, administration/management was found to be the category of LIS jobs with the largest number of postings. This category was also found to be one of the top earning jobs, with most of the positions earning upwards of $100,000 annually. As these jobs are library-related and require specific knowledge of library services, most of them also require ALA-accredited MLIS degrees. Or, at the very least, these degrees are useful, as candidates with MLIS degrees have a greater understanding of libraries and will thus likely have an advantage when on the job.

A job in the library management and development field entails:

  • Planning, managing, cultivating, and securing philanthropic support through fundraising campaigns, donor giving, and program support
  • Fiscal and operational oversight, including strategic direction and implementation of organization’s strategic plan, creating impact reports and proposals, and keeping in mind operational budgets and resource utilization
  • Community relations, including determining community priorities, representing the library/organization, leading community engagement
  • Grow donor prospects, build and maintain donor relationships and engagement
  • Maintain board relationships
  • Develop fundraising campaigns, execute fundraising events
  • Publicity, communications, and marketing of development efforts
  • Oversees and guides staff, provides mentorship
  • Database management

Sample job descriptions in the library management/development sector:

  • Executive Director, Houston Public Library Foundation (HPLF)
  • Executive Director, Chillicothe & Ross County Public Library
  • Assistant Director of Development, University Library, California State University, Long Beach
  • Development Administrative Assistant, Library Foundation of Los Angeles

Professional Context

Joining and networking through professional associations depends on what sector you prefer to work in. Executive Directors of Library Foundations can work in Public Libraries, Academic Libraries, and even School Libraries. All of these institutions need outside funding and fundraising, especially now when federal funding is being significantly cut. While there aren't many professional associations for this specialization in particular, there are a multitude of general associations worth looking into:

  • American Library Association (ALA)
  • Public Library Association (PLA)
  • California Library Association (CLA)
  • Association of Fundraising Professionals (AFP)

If you are interested in pursuing a job in library development, library fundraising, or anything related to library foundations, conferences can be a great place to meet people and potentially hear about open positions. Some conferences that professionals in this specialization might attend include:

  • United For Libraries Virtual
  • International Public Library Fundraising Conference

Job-market Outlook

Going into this project, our assumption was that the field would be growing slowly, though potentially hindered within the past two years due to federal cuts to GLAM institutions. However, our talk with Lauren cleared up some worries and shed new light on the rise of exciting opportunities within information institutions. Lauren touched on the common presumption that automated procedures performed by self checkout kiosks and check in machines remove reference librarians from their positions, and revealed they instead free up time for librarians to perform more hands-on tasks for users. Now, libraries are able to allocate more positions to coordinators and directors, allowing for increased community support through a wide variety of specialized programs. Lauren emphasized how there is more room for jobs within libraries that are not strictly labeled as “librarian” positions, such as her own as Executive Director, and even social work. Lauren also shared she does not believe AI will threaten jobs, and instead, can allow for more positions where librarians can become custodians of such programs.

In our research, we found that our initial assumption was somewhat correct; library positions are slowly but steadily on the rise, though specific roles vary in percentages. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that the field is expected to grow by 2 percent from 2024 to 2034, which is slower than all other fields. This report notes the growing need for librarians who can serve users beyond traditional borrowing methods, which is consistent with Lauren’s experience. Additionally, the Department for Professional Employees’s Facts & Figures for Library Professionals shows employment among librarians increased more than 27 percent in 2024, and employment among librarian technicians remained the same. Employment for library assistants decreased, and though there is not enough research done to figure out the cause, we suspect this is due to an increase in library professionals who have MLIS degrees, and therefore hold higher positions within their institutions. As well, the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics’s Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics Profile for Administrative Service Managers, such as Lauren, shows California is the state with the highest employment for such roles, with Southern California being the second largest metropolitan area in the United States for employment in the field. Those who are seeking positions can utilize such reports in order to evaluate job markets, and make plans for the future. It is worth noting that Lauren identified our concern for current DEI funding cuts impacting the viability of our line of work, and she emphasized our positionality as library professionals in California, and how imperative it is to continue on to support colleagues and users in more heavily affected states.

Technical Skills Snapshot

Looking to follow this path yourself? Our research and Lauren’s expertise can provide a number of tips and knowledge points for anyone interested in similar roles. As an LIS professional, and a leader in particular, it’s vital that you be prepared to take on a number of different roles at once, meaning your skillset should be just as varied. Strong organizational skills, an understanding of technologies such as the Customer Relationship Management (CRM system), data + statistical management, financial planning, and community understanding were some of the strongest contenders for necessary skills in the field. Community engagement especially is a useful skill to hone based not only on our interview but our research into other library directors. A 2021 study on the role of LIS professionals in local systems placed a strong focus on the importance of understanding community needs in the role. Because LIS professionals work closely with patrons from all walks of life, it’s crucial to learn how to plan and organize events for specific communities and invest time in understanding varying stakeholders.

Most notably, though, our interview highlighted a key skill necessary for new professionals that is often overlooked in MLIS programs: public speaking and communication. The ability to connect with staff, institutional partners, and the communities your institution serves is one of the strongest ways you can stand out in a role. A 2011 article covering the most useful knowledge bases of library directors noted people skills and coordination as the most prominent skills professionals rely on. Over a decade later, a 2025 study on the path to leadership in libraries found similar results, deeming the most important qualities of a successful director candidate to be interpersonal and emotional intelligence, communication and collaboration skills, and fundraising and grant writing knowledge.

For the modern LIS professional, though, technological skills are becoming a larger point of discussion than ever before with the rise of AI. GLAM institutions across the world are gradually adopting AI and their own policies around the technology, so what does this mean for professionals and leaders? For as long as libraries have existed in the tech world, a basic understanding of computers and databases has been required. In January 2026, the Journal of Academic Librarianship published a piece on the ways in which these roles are requiring AI integration for both employees and patrons, meaning there is some requirement for new professionals to have a baseline understanding of AI use as well. In Lauren’s experience, AI is used at the discretion of each individual and can support tasks like data analysis and organization which are required in day-to-day activities. Interestingly enough, few job listings even refer to AI or its uses in the requirements or description sections, indicating that this is a skill still under consideration for relevance.

And remember: as Lauren points out, a job listing has a wish list, not a demands list. Learning on the job is always possible. See below for a few example job listings highlighting the skills we’ve discussed here:

  • Executive Director Riverside
  • Library Director ALA
  • Assistant Director UCLA
  • Library Director Santa Barbara

Labor Trends

Based on conducted research and talking with Lauren, this field is not a high focus for folks who are getting an MLIS. Lauren herself unintentionally fell into the field as a temporary option, not expecting to stay there long. This mindset often limits the focus placed on recruitment in this area. When attempting to look for opportunities to get experience in library management and/or development, there weren’t many internships available at the moment. Most are focused on entry-level public librarianship, metadata, archives, or data entry. However, there are still many opportunities to get experience outside of what would be considered “library studies.” Lauren discussed many of the tasks she currently does include marketing, fundraising, budgeting, etc. which can all be transferable skills gained by working at nonprofits or other organizations that are heavily involved in administrative work. These experiences, though, are likely to be unpaid or offer low pay. Once in the field, positions are often available on a limited term, with contracts making up a large percentage of archivist work; as Lauren pointed out, this makes management work a more stable alternative to typical desired career paths..

One major point Lauren made to us during the interview was that people in the field, especially new MLIS graduates, need to be able to advocate for themselves in regards to income and benefits. For example, she mentioned the need to be able to negotiate your professional development benefits, suggesting making sure that your job will pay for you to go to conferences such as ALA. Like in any profession, it’s important that you learn to speak up for yourself when being offered a job. Even within this field, there are still issues of equitable compensation, especially with the amount of physical, emotional, and mental labor put into it.

Lastly, some of the more general issues within LIS work is a lack of racial and ethnic diversity, according to the Department for Professional Employees. This is seen in most specializations and is likely even more prevalent in management positions. Some solutions being implemented in the field to promote diversity are through programs like Spectrum by ALA and REFORMA. These programs aim to support librarians of color or those seeking to serve diverse communities by providing financial support, networking opportunities, and more. Lauren further discussed the focus on care gradually becoming more common among LIS professionals, with creating community being her biggest suggestion for new professionals to combat the unstable landscape of the field.

Training Agenda

While technical skills can come from any number of experiences, we’ll use this space to highlight what we found to be the most beneficial steps new professionals can take to be successful in LIS leadership roles. As previously discussed, we found that public-facing communication skills, community involvement, and data management were among the most valuable skills for budding professionals to gain. So the question is, how do they learn these skills? The first step is to get practical experience wherever possible: this can include internships, class projects, volunteer work, mentorship , or even personal projects with guidance from online sources. Our expert Lauren noted that there is no one way to gain experience—her own trajectory went from archival intern to customer service representative at a museum, leaving lots of room for alternative paths in between. What matters most is gaining transferable skills and learning to apply them to your resume. In Lauren’s case, working in customer service taught her how to handle finances, manage inventory data, and developed her professional communication skills. Even your part-time campus job can provide avenues for this development when you analyze the tasks you do on the daily.

However, internships and volunteer work come with barriers. For many students, unpaid work is not an option, their time is limited due to personal and educational commitments, and a lack of representation in the field can create obstacles for BIPOC students as demonstrated by multiple studies. A case study out of the 2025 LIS Conference at Loyola Marymount University covers many of these issues, as well as the skills students can build when they have adequate access to internships. There are, however, a number of opportunities outside of work experience that can help build your skills. For example, student associations like the Society of American Archivists or UCLA chapter of the American Library Association are great ways to practice public speaking, learn to fundraise in a professional setting, manage data through meeting minutes and records, and plan events. Conferences can be a great source of networking, as making connections is vital to building your career, and they also host helpful workshops that teach you skills you may not learn in school. In the MLIS program, taking classes on subjects like cataloguing and appraisal sets you up with management tools you can use in any LIS profession. Our research suggests taking courses in statistics and data management specifically which, as supported by Lauren’s experience, can be immensely helpful in creating versatile job candidates. Certifications like Lauren’s Certified Fund Raising Executive (CRFE) or the Certified Public Library Administrator (CPLA) are alternative routes you can take to boost your credentials.

Five ways to understand the profession

Dilworth, Kathryn. 2021. “Philanthropy in Public Libraries: Its Impact on Community Well-Being Missions.” International Journal of Community Well-Being, August. https://doi.org/10.1007/s42413-021-00140-8.

Kathryn Dilworth’s journal article “Philanthropy in Public Libraries: Its Impact on Community Well-Being Missions” examines funding for public libraries, and the impact of philanthropy on their ability to fulfill community needs. Dilworth’s sample pool consists of four public library directors in Mississippi who are in similar roles to Lauren’s, and through this study, she uncovers the importance of outside funding for public libraries to continue operating. The article uncovers a lack of professional training in fundraising among library directors and staff, and calls for libraries to utilize philanthropy networks more in order to meet community needs. This article is useful in demonstrating the importance of communication skills and networking for MLIS students hoping to enter management roles, as being able to advocate for more funds is a crucial element of the job.
