Welcome & Introduction

Welcome to our github highlighting the work of LIS professionals, specifically in the Library Studies specialization, focusing on library/organization management. This project was conducted by Anna Friedman, Diana Naughton, Emma Hughes, and Sam Garcia for IS 270: Systems and Infrastructures. In order to gain insight into the field and assess how AI is impacting Library and Information Science (LIS) work, we conducted an interview with Lauren Trujillo and conducted secondary research highlighting the profession. The following is a compiled collection and analysis of our findings.

Interview

              

Lauren Trujillo serves as the Executive Director of the Santa Barbara Public Library Foundation, where she has now been for 7 years. She previously worked solely in fundraising for the public library in the form of capital and campaigns as Foundation Director. She later transitioned to the role of Executive Director where she "wears all the hats" including fundraising, marketing, board liason, tech and IT, AI consultant, etc. Here department has only 3 staff the works to raise anywhere from $1 million to $6 million each year.

Professional Context

Joining and networking through professional associations is going to depend on what sector you prefer to work in. Executive Directors of Library Foundations can work in Public Libraries, Academic Libraries, and even School Libraries. All of these need outside funding and fundraising, especially now when federal funding is being significantly cut. While there aren't many specialized professional associations for this specialization in particular, there are a multitude of general associations worth looking into including:

  • American Library Association (ALA)
  • Public Library Association (PLA)
  • California Library Association (CLA)
  • Association of Fundraising Professionals (AFP)

If you are interested in pursuing a job in library development, library fundraising, or anything related to library foundations, conferences could be a great place to meet people and potentially hear about open positions. Some conferences that professionals in this specialization might attend include:

  • American Library Association Conference
  • AFP ICON
  • United For Libraries Virtual
  • International Public Library Fundraising Conference

If you are new to the field and want to gain more insight into the culture and trends of this specialization, reading literature can be a great starting point. Below you can find some journal articles that speak on this topic:

  • "Strength in Numbers: How Donors, Friends, and Volunteers Drive Library Advocacy"
  • "Desperately Seeking Funding: Library Guides to Student Funding"
  • Many universities publish their own journals regarding their libraries or research being done at their own institutions regarding library development.

Need a place to start looking for jobs and opportunities? There are various spaces you can begin looking at, however narrowing your search onto the specific areas you are looking for is most helpful, such as archives, public libraries, school libraries, etc. These are some great resources that post LIS positions:

  • LinkedIn
  • Library Websites
  • INALJ Jobs
  • Archives Gig
  • JobLIST
  • CLA Career Website
  • code4lib jobs

Labor Trends

Based on conducted research and talking with Lauren, it seems this field is not a high focus for folks who are getting an MLIS. Lauren mentioned kind of just unintentionally falling into the field as a temporary option, not expecting to stay there long. This mindset often limits the focus placed on recruitment in this area. When attempting to look for opportunities to get experience in library management and/or development, there weren’t many internships available at the moment. Most are focused on entry-level public librarianship, metadata, archives, or data entry. However, there are still many opportunities to get experience outside of what would be considered “library studies.” Lauren mentions many of the tasks she currently does include marketing, fundraising, budgeting, etc. which can all be transferable skills gained with working at nonprofits or other organizations that are heavily involved in administrative work. These experiences, though, are likely to be unpaid or offer low pay.

One huge point Lauren made to us during the interview was that people in the field, especially new MLIS graduates, need to be able to advocate for themselves in regards to income and benefits. For example, she mentioned how you need to be able to negotiate your professional development benefits, suggesting making sure that your job will pay for you to go to conferences, such as ALA. Like in any profession, it’s important that you speak up for yourself when being offered a job. Even within this field, there are still issues of equitable compensation, especially with the amount of physical, emotional, and mental labor put into it.

Lastly, some of the more general issues within LIS work is a lack of racial and ethnic diversity, according to the Department for Professional Employees. This is seen in most specializations and is likely even more prevalent in management positions, like one that Lauren is in. Some solutions being implemented in the field to try and diversify it are through programs like Spectrum by ALA and REFORMA. These programs aim to support librarians of color or those seeking to serve diverse communities by providing financial support, networking opportunities, and more.
